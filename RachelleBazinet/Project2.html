Introduction and Academic Biography

Patricia Smith Churchland is a Canadian-American known for taking a neuroscientific view of philosophy's classic mind-body problem. This position is called Eliminative Materialism, which postulates the ultimate reduction of the entirety of the folk psychology framework to the neuroscientific underpinnings of the brain. Churchland earned her Bachelor’s degree in Arts at the University of British Columbia and continued her education and obtained her Master’s degree in Arts at the University of Pittsburg (Braund, 2008). She then studied at Oxford University and gained her Bachelor of Philosophy, a post-graduate degree involving a supervised research project or a thesis. She has taught at many different universities throughout the years and continues to teach today. In her career, the neurophilosopher gained numerous awards and has had many publications (Braund, 2008).

I've chosen five of Patricia Churland’s videos from YouTube that provide a thorough overview of her academic contributions and role in the field of cognitive science. Churland is a strong supporter of interdisciplinary research and collaboration, a commitment that is reflected in her focus on neurophilosophy. Despite lacking a formal neuroscience degree, she displays a keen interest in unravelling the intricacies of the brain's mechanisms. Churland has amassed significant expertise in this area and uses it to answer the mysterious phenomena behind folk psychological notions like free will and morality. Patricia Churchland on Eliminative Materialism

58,934 views - 9:22 minutes

In the video titled "Patricia Churchland on Eliminative Materialism," Churchland discusses the naturalization of the mind, tracing its roots back to Aristotle and highlighting its evolution with 19th-century psychologists and neuroscientists. She asserts that current data strongly indicate the exclusive role of the physical brain in mental processes, dismissing the existence of a non-physical mind or soul. Churchland advocates for collaboration between philosophers, neuroscientists, and psychologists, suggesting that advancements in neuroscience compel a reevaluation of folk psychology. Drawing parallels to changes in folk physics with Newtonian physics, she posits that aspects of folk psychology, such as the unified concept of memory and attention, may need revision based on neuroscience findings. Churchland predicts a transformation in understanding decision-making and emphasizes that a deeper scientific grasp of neurobiology enhances rather than diminishes our comprehension of mental phenomena. She likens this shift to the redefinition of light as electromagnetic radiation, highlighting that a neurobiological perspective enriches our understanding without erasing the richness of human experiences like love.

Neurophilosophy and free will

31,656 views - 16:01 minutes

Patricia Churchland's YouTube video "Neurophilosophy and Free Will" delves into the concept of free will and its implications in the context of neurobiology. Churchland explores the concept and its association with being in control of one's actions and ties this to the development of the criminal law system in various societies, emphasizing the criteria for holding individuals responsible for their actions. She delves into the philosophical debate surrounding free will, considering whether true control is possible if our decisions are influenced by the conditions of the brain, which is often viewed as a causal machine. The speaker also discusses self-control in mammals, drawing parallels between animal behaviour and human tendencies. Churchland touches upon the study of self-control in rats, the impact of motivation, and how drugs can undermine self-control. Additionally, she explores the neurobiology of addiction, emphasizing the role of stress hormones. The discussion extends to the adolescent brain and its inclination towards risk-taking, posing questions about self-control and its implications in the criminal law system. Churchland expresses her perspective on free will and suggests that understanding causality in self-control is crucial.

Neurophilosophy

28,208 views - 14:04 minutes

In her YouTube video “Neurophilosophy”, Patricia Churchland addresses the connection between neuroscience and philosophy. She introduces the concept of neurophilosophy, which explores how discoveries in neuroscience can contribute to understanding fundamental questions about the mind. Churchland shares a pivotal early discovery that motivated her interest in the field—the observation of split-brain patients, whose cerebral hemispheres were surgically separated to control epilepsy. This separation led to distinct consciousness experiences in each hemisphere, revealing a connection between the physical brain and consciousness. Churchland emphasizes the profound impact of neuroscience on unravelling the nature of learning and memory, citing studies on patients with hippocampal damage and rodent spatial learning. She delves into the various levels of organization in neuroscience, from molecules to neural circuits, highlighting the ongoing challenges in understanding the brain's complexity. Churchland also touches on the potential implications of neuroscience for addressing medical issues like schizophrenia and Alzheimer's, and she suggests that advancements may lead to innovative technologies. Churchland underscores the critical and interdisciplinary nature of neurophilosophy, bridging gaps between philosophical inquiry and scientific exploration.

The Brains Behind Morality | TEDxNorrköping

8,163 views - 19:16 minutes

Patricia Churchland's TED talk, "The Brains Behind Morality," explores the origins of moral motivation and values, challenging the traditional link between morality and religion. Contrary to the belief that organized religions dictate moral norms, Churchland traces the roots of morality back to evolution, specifically in warm-blooded creatures' emergence. This evolutionary process led to increased intelligence and parental care, fostering altruistic behaviours and strong parent-offspring bonds. Churchland emphasizes the role of oxytocin in maternal bonding and social connections, influenced by ecological factors and oxytocin receptor distribution. She explores how children acquire moral norms through social inclusion, cooperation, storytelling, and the impact of natural opioids on reinforcing prosocial behaviours. While neuroscience provides insights into the neural mechanisms of morality, Churchland acknowledges that it doesn't offer definitive answers to moral dilemmas. Human deliberation, negotiation, and common sense remain essential in addressing complex ethical issues. In essence, Churchland's presentation illuminates morality's evolutionary and neurological foundations, recognizing ongoing human responsibility in making moral choices.

What is Frank Jackson's Mary Argument? The Royal Institute of Philosophy

4,200 views - 16:18 minutes

In this YouTube video, Patricia Churchland explores the Mary Argument, a philosophical thought experiment proposed by Frank Jackson. The scenario involves Mary, a scientist who knows all the physical information about colour vision in the brain but has never experienced colour. The argument suggests that Mary would learn something new upon seeing colour, challenging physicalism's claim that all experiences are governed by physical rules. Churchland criticizes the argument by highlighting the significance of pathways, developmental routes, and genetic factors in sensory experiences like vision and taste. She questions the causal relation between the physical brain and consciousness, challenging Jackson's argument and its compatibility with neuroscience and genetics. Churchland discusses the importance of specific neural pathways, referencing studies on congenitally blind children gaining sight through surgery. She emphasizes the role of developmental routes and challenges the idea that knowing everything about a physical process guarantees the corresponding experience. Churchland concludes by questioning the causal relation between the physical brain and consciousness, pointing out the lack of answers from proponents of non-physical theories like Jackson's.

Analysis of the Content

Having delved into several of Patricia Churchland's widely viewed videos, it becomes evident that this renowned scientist is distinguished for her insightful discussions. She places significant emphasis on unravelling the neurobiological intricacies of the brain and its capacity to explain profound philosophical inquiries surrounding consciousness, morality, and free will. While my perspectives on her scholarly contributions are somewhat ambivalent, I confidently assert that she exhibits a profound understanding of the topics covered in the videos, substantiating her arguments convincingly. My evaluation of her work can be compartmentalized into two distinct facets: the calibre of the content and my subjective opinions about the content.

To commence, I want to emphasize the high quality of content in Churchland's videos. She excels in elucidating seemingly intricate concepts with remarkable clarity. Topics that might be presented by other professors in a convoluted and theory-laden manner become comprehensible and logical through her explanations. The key lies in her approach to presenting content. Following each video, one can easily discern a discernible pattern in how she tackles philosophical inquiries using neuroscience. She initiates by connecting the question to commonplace situations in everyday society, as evident in her discussion on neurophilosophy where she parallels the notion of free will with the justice system. This strategy is particularly beneficial for those unfamiliar with philosophy in an academic context, making her content accessible to a wide audience. After establishing a connection to everyday scenarios, she succinctly defines the fundamental concepts addressed in the video, laying the groundwork for her arguments—a crucial aspect of philosophical analysis. Subsequently, Churchland articulates her thesis related to the subject, consistently tying it back to neuroscience. To fortify her thesis, she offers illustrative empirical examples, primarily drawing from scientific studies or case studies in neuroscience.

Upon scrutinizing the structural aspects of her argumentation, the subsequent phase of this analysis involves evaluating the substance of her arguments. As mentioned earlier, my perspective on her philosophical stance is nuanced. Upon a comprehensive examination of Patricia Churchland's central thesis regarding the origins of morality, I find myself aligning with her viewpoint. She adeptly elucidates how evolution shapes the human brain, impacting interpersonal behaviour. Churchland posits that morality is an evolutionarily advantageous trait, ensuring its perpetuation across generations. She effectively conveys the pivotal role of the brain in morality, utilizing illustrative examples to underscore the significance of oxytocin in fostering altruistic behaviour. It is rational to conclude that the evolutionary benefits of parent-offspring bonding extend to societal interactions, contributing to the survival of our species. Both of Churchland's arguments are persuasive, rooted in robust logical reasoning and substantial evidential support.

Conversely, my engagement with the "Mary's Argument" video was less profound. It dismisses the thought experiment's goal, opting instead to delve into the workings of the visual pathway in the brain. Philosopher Frank Jackson introduced "Mary's Argument" due to the inherently elusive nature of personal experiences of the world. The qualia, or the qualities of experiencing red or tasting something sour, remain mysteriously unexplained by research. Jackson contends that Mary, the colour-blind scientist, would gain new insights upon experiencing the colour red for the first time. Despite not being a Cartesian Dualist, I concur with this assertion. Churchland attempts to counter this by delving into the neural mechanisms of vision, but this falls short of addressing the qualia associated with seeing red. Merely understanding the mechanical functions behind abstract phenomena like love or perception fails to encompass the lived experience, emotions, and sensations accompanying these occurrences.

In a general overview, the chosen five videos offer a comprehensive portrayal of Patricia Churchland's role in the field of cognitive science. Churchland predominantly leans on neurological mechanisms as the cornerstone for addressing a wide array of inquiries about the brain and the mind. Although her method of argumentation is robust and unified, she faces scrutiny for her inflexible stance on concepts such as free will and consciousness. Overall Churchland exhibits an in-depth understanding of the topics discussed in the videos, presenting well-structured arguments. Her notable academic background underscores her ongoing commitment to unravelling some of philosophy's most profound questions.
