1. Csikszentmihalyi’s perspective

Firstly, a key part of Csikszentmihalyi’s idea of flow involves understanding what contributes to a life that is worth living and the "roots of happiness".

As shown in Figure 1, which Csikszentmihalyi discussed in the podcast, after a certain basic point, increases in material well-being do not seem to affect

how happy people are. Flow is essentially an analogy for a mental state where you do not feel like you are doing your ordinary routines. An example that he provides is

writing a poem. When writing a poem, a poet often experiences a state of flow: whole parts of the poem "arrive" in the poet’s mind all at once.

Figure 1: Personal income vs happiness

Csikszentmihalyi identified the 7 main steps of Flow. These are:

  • 1. Completely involved in what we are doing – focused, concentrated.
  • 2. A sense of ecstasy – of being outside everyday reality.
  • 3. Great inner clarity – knowing what needs to be done, and how well we are doing.
  • 4. Knowing that the activity is doable – that our skills are adequate to the task.
  • 5. A sense of serenity – no worries about oneself, and a feeling of growing beyond the boundaries of the ego.
  • 6. Timelessness – thoroughly focused on the present, hours seem to pass by in minutes.
  • 7. Intrinsic motivation – whatever produces flow becomes its own reward.

2. Agree/disagree with his perspective

I agree with his perspective. I have often experienced flow myself when playing video games. This experience can be described in several ways: losing track of time

(step 6 in the list above), heightened focus on a task (step 1), and losing track of your surroundings (step 2) (Zubek, 2020).

Games are designed to create a state of flow. They achieve this in several ways (Kraut et al, 2012, Chapter 2):

  • Making games challenging but not overwhelming (step 4)
  • Setting clear goals for and providing feedback in games (step 3)
  • Supporting social interaction in games (step 5)

In particular, making a game challenging is central to the idea of flow. As a player masters the game, learning to play the game should be fun.

The level of skill should increase with the experience of the player (Zubek, 2020). The game should neither be too easy nor too difficult.

Flow is also used to create intrinsic motivation (step 7): gaining mastery of a game is inherently interesting to players (Zubek, 2020).

3. Alternatives

A somewhat different view is presented by Koster (2004), as cited by Zubek (2020).

He argues that players will experience fun when they are able to "predict outcomes" within the game, for example navigating a difficult level

in a platform game. As Zubek argues, this perspective comes from the standpoint of game design rather than cognitive science.

However, the implication of this perspective is similar to the flow perspective. The game’s level of challenge has to keep increasing with the player’s skill level.

This has to be done in such a way that the increases in difficulty are both challenging and doable for the player.

Another alternative explanation is that players are mainly extrinsically motivated, not intrinsically motivated Zubek, 2020).

A point to support this argument would leaderboards used in many online games which encourage players to compete against each other for

higher positions as well as for prizes. In this case, players are not primarily motivated by learning the game mechanics, but by receiving a reward,

such a prize or special status.
